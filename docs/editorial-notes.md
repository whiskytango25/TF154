# Reaper Six — Editorial Notes and Style Guidance

---

## CORE EDITORIAL PRINCIPLE

This is not a military action thriller. It escalates morally, not kinetically. Any editorial decision that pushes toward more gunfights, more action, or faster kinetic pacing is working against the book.

The slow institutional dread is the book. Protect it.

---

## VOICE AND POV

### Narrative POV
- Close third-person, always anchored in a character's consciousness
- No omniscient narrator — the reader only knows what the POV character knows
- Primary POV: Hale
- Secondary POVs: Cricket (Ch3, Ch10 notably), Brick (Ch11), Padre (Ch5, Ch25)
- Wraith: **no POV access** — this is deliberate. His interiority is restricted. The absence is the character.

### The Narrator's Register
- Deliberate, architecturally constructed sentences
- Controlled repetition of specific words/phrases is intentional — "specific", "filed", "column" are load-bearing voice architecture
- Do not strip the house voice. Reduce clusters of overused words, not the words themselves.
- The narrator observes what characters register, not what they feel — emotions are processed, filed, or converted to tactical categories

---

## CHARACTER VOICE DIFFERENTIATION

### Hale
- **Architectural.** Builds frameworks. Reads rooms. Processes threat geometry into philosophical structure.
- Subordinate clauses. Careful sentence construction.
- Dark humor as armor — gets quieter under pressure, not louder.
- Interior: constructs understanding of systems and people through layered observation

### Brick
- **Declarative.** Files. Judges. Compresses.
- Subject-verb-object. No subordinate architecture.
- Thinks in units of judgment and consequence.
- His interior, when rendered, should use short hard sentences — not Hale's subordinate clause style.
- "North." One word. The whole decision.
- **Critical:** Brick's dialogue is correct throughout. His interior POV sections are where voice drift occurs — fix there.

### Cricket
- **Analytical.** Pattern recognition. Systems language. Self-diagnostics.
- Relabels emotional responses as operational categories.
- "He recognized it, noted it, applied the standard countermeasure: the picture was not complete. Continue."
- Does not think in metaphors or hand-images — thinks in compatibility questions, binary assessments, flag/continue logic.
- "Continue." as a directive he issues himself — this is the correct register.

### Padre
- **Theological precision applied to practical things.**
- Makes exact distinctions. Doesn't linger on atmosphere.
- His thinking has the structure of someone trained for the priesthood who left — systematic, specific, repurposed.
- Does not linger on warmth — he notes it, files it, moves.
- The desert doesn't have opinions. It exists without reference to what's happening in the building.

### Gremlin
- Dialogue is correct throughout.
- His best lines are clean exits from scenes — the contrast with rougher dialogue is what makes them land.
- 30% of his non-key dialogue should be rough, throwaway, unfinished.
- Do not give him too many clean lines — the ratio matters.

### Wraith
- Says almost nothing.
- Every word he says has weight.
- No POV sections.
- His silence is a feature, not a gap.

## PROSE STYLE RULES

### Sentence density
- The opening three chapters (Prologue, Ch1, Ch2) should run slightly compressed relative to later chapters.
- Reflective subordinate clauses in the opening should be trimmed at the clause level — not ideas or scenes, clauses.
- The observation should do the work; the elaborating clause that explains what the observation already communicated is the cut target.

### What to cut:
- **"Which was X"** clauses that explain what the image already showed
- **"The kind of Y that Z"** elaborations after a clean image
- **"The specific [adjective]"** when "specific" is doing nothing but adding weight
- The explanation of what a character's silence communicated — let the silence communicate it

### What to keep:
- Controlled repetition of Hale's processing vocabulary
- The house rhythm of "filed", "registered", "noted" — these are structural, not stylistic overuse
- Long sentences where the length is doing work (the authorization structure passage is an example)
- Short declarative sentences for Brick's interior — the contrast with the surrounding prose is intentional

### The chapter-ending bridge notes:
- The `> *Next: Chapter X*` teasers are intentional structural choices — they build dread between chapters
- **Keep:** "The terminal. A name. His hand stops on the mouse."
- **Keep:** "Overnight. Nobody sent the orders."
- **Review:** Earlier bridge notes that read as outline cards rather than atmosphere — reduce those
- Do not wholesale remove. They function as interstitials.

## WHAT TO PROTECT (DO NOT CHANGE)

- "The dog with his sovereignty" — keep. It's good writing.
- Chapter-ending bridge notes — selective review only, not wholesale removal
- The Confessor underdevelopment — series architecture, Book 2
- Glossary exists for civilian accessibility — keep it
- Hale's exceptionalism level — don't reduce it in Book 1; let the cost show across the series
- "specific" as a word — reduce clusters, don't strip the house voice
- Wraith's interiority restriction — the absence is the character

---

## WHAT TO REJECT FROM EXTERNAL EDITORIAL NOTES

- **More professional disagreement / rank tension / team conflict** — wrong template. These men have metabolized friction. The absence of overt conflict IS characterization.
- **"Hale exceptionalism reduce 15%"** — his exceptionalism is correct. The cost is a Book 2+ problem.
- **Civilian confusion about power centers** — this is pacing, not structure. Fix the density; the architecture resolves itself.
- **Make this faster, louder, more kinetic** — this is the wrong book. The slow dread is the book.
